Temporal Difference Calibration in Sequential Tasks: Application to Vision-Language-Action Models

Anonymous Authors • ICML 2026 Submission

READ PAPER VIEW ON GITHUB

Abstract

Recent advances in vision-language-action (VLA) models for robotics have highlighted the importance of reliable uncertainty quantification in sequential tasks. However, assessing and improving calibration in such settings remains mostly unexplored, especially when only partial trajectories are observed. In this work, we formulate sequential calibration for episodic tasks, where task-success confidence is produced along an episode, while success is determined at the end of it. We introduce a sequential extension of the Brier score and show that, for binary outcomes, its risk minimizer coincides with the VLA policy’s value function. This connection bridges uncertainty calibration and reinforcement learning, enabling the use of temporal-difference (TD) value estimation as a principled calibration mechanism over time. We empirically show that TD calibration improves performance relative to the state-of-the-art on simulated and real-robot data. Interestingly, we show that when calibrated using TD, the VLA's single-step action probabilities can yield competitive uncertainty estimates, in contrast to recent findings that employed different calibration techniques.

1   Introduction

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have rapidly emerged as a powerful paradigm for generalist robot manipulation, combining the rich semantic understanding of large language models with sensorimotor control. Despite impressive task-completion rates, deploying these models in safety-critical settings requires knowing how confident the model is that the current trajectory will succeed - and that confidence must be calibrated.

Standard post-hoc calibration (e.g., Platt scaling, temperature scaling) was designed for i.i.d. classification. In robotics, however, observations arrive as a temporally correlated sequence and the outcome label, binary task success, is revealed only at the end. This raises a fundamental question: how to reason about calibration in sequential tasks?

  • Temporal inconsistency: the confidence in the current decision may depend on the confidence of future decisions.
  • Label scarcity: the ground-truth label is typically delayed.

We address both problems with TDQC. Our main conceptual insight shows that, when formulating a sequential task as a partially-observable Markov decision process, we can reduce the problem of minimizing the sequential Brier score to the problem of learning a value function. The global risk minimizer of this loss is exactly the state-value function \(Q^\pi\), connecting calibration theory to reinforcement learning in a principled way.

TDQC   Our paper further advances the research in five key areas:

Sequential Calibration Framework

We provide a coherent formulation for calibration in sequential tasks, unifying recent VLA failure-detection work with the broader calibration literature.

  RL Connection

We link sequential calibration to value prediction in reinforcement learning and derive a novel TD-based calibration method, TDQC.

Black-Box Practicality

Using only action probabilities, TDQC matches or improves SAFE variants that require hidden-state access, which is often unavailable through APIs.

Empirical Strength

TD-based losses are essential for state-of-the-art early detection on LIBERO with OpenVLA, π₀, and π₀-FAST, and on a Franka real-robot dataset collected with π₀-FAST.

Policy Improvement as a By-Product

The TDQC value predictor can guide action selection within a set of actions sampled from the policy, yielding a simple recipe that brings a 15% increase in success rate of OpenVLA on the LIBERO benchmark.

2   Method

2.1   Problem Setting

Let \(h_t\) denote the history of observations and actions up to time \(t\) in a trajectory of length \(T\), and let \(y \in \{0,1\}\) denote final task success. We wish to learn a function \(f_\theta : \mathcal{H} \to [0,1]\) such that \(f_\theta(h_t)\) is a calibrated estimate of \(\Pr[\text{success} \mid h_t]\) at every step.

2.2   Sequential Brier Score & the Value-Function Connection

The standard per-step Brier score \(\mathbb{E}[(f_\theta(x) - y)^2]\), where \(x\) denotes the input, ignores temporal structure. We instead minimize the sequential Brier score - the sum of squared prediction errors across all time-steps. Crucially, this loss telescopes:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{seq}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{t=1}^{T}(f_\theta(h_t) - y)^2 \big| h_t\right]$$

The unique minimizer satisfies \(f_\theta(h_t) = \mathbb{E}[y \mid h_t]\), i.e., the policy's value function \(V^\pi(h_t)\). This motivates the TDQC training objective:

$$G(\theta) = \sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\!\bigl(f_\theta(h_t) - f_\theta(h_{t+1})\bigr)^2 \;+\; \bigl(f_\theta(h_T) - y\bigr)^2$$

TDQC Loss - TD consistency terms (left) plus a terminal supervision term (right).

The left terms enforce temporal consistency along the trajectory, while the terminal term anchors the final prediction to the ground-truth label.

2.3   TDQC Architecture

We parameterize \(f_\theta\) as the SAFE paper - a recurrent model that consumes the hidden states of the frozen VLA backbone at each step and outputs a success probability:

  • SAFE-MLP: A shallow MLP applied independently at each step (ablation baseline). with cumulative losses.
  • SAFE-MLP-BCE: A shallow MLP applied independently at each step (ablation baseline) with binary cross-entropy loss.
  • SAFE-LSTM: An LSTM that processes the full history \(h_t\), enabling temporally coherent predictions.
  • SAFE-LSTM-TD0: The same LSTM trained with the full TDQC loss \(G(\theta)\) - our best-performing variant.
  • TDQC-BCE: A sequential model that processes the action probabilities and is trained with a binary cross-entropy objective.
  • TDQC-TD0: A sequential model that processes the action probabilities and is trained with the full TDQC loss \(G(\theta)\).

2.4   Training Procedure

Algorithm 1  -  TDQC Training
Input: policy \(\pi\), calibration dataset \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}} = \{(h_T^i, y^i)\}_{i=1}^N\)
1 for until convergence do
2 Sample \(h_T^i\) uniformly from \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}\)
3 Compute the TD loss:
\(G(\theta) = \sum\limits_{t=1}^{T-1}(f_\theta(h_t^i) - f_\theta(h_{t+1}^i))^2 + (f_\theta(h_T^i) - y^i)^2\)
4 Update \(\theta \leftarrow \theta - \alpha\,\nabla G(\theta)\)
5 end for

3   Experiments

3.1   Setup

We evaluate on three robot manipulation benchmarks:

  • LIBERO-10 - a suite of tabletop manipulation tasks in simulation, split into seen and unseen task distributions to test generalization.
  • WidowX - lifting and pick-and-place tasks on a physical WidowX robot manipulator.
  • Franka Real-Robot - pick-and-place tasks on a physical Franka Emika arm.

Across these benchmarks we evaluate four frozen VLA backbones: OpenVLA, UniVLA,π₀, and π₀-FAST. Calibration functions are trained only on trajectories from the seen split and evaluated on the held-out unseen split to measure out-of-distribution generalization. We report ROC-AUC (how well a score ranks failed rollouts above successful ones, higher is better) and Brier Score (calibration quality, lower is better).
